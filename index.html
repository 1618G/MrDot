Born in Portsmouth and diagnosed with Retinitis Pigmentosa, Clarke Reynolds lost his sight but never his artistic vision. Today, he pioneers a unique form of art that turns Braille into bold, colorful, touchable creations for both sighted and visually impaired audiences.

Early Life & Vision Loss

Clarke Reynolds was born with limited vision in his right eye. In his 30s, he was diagnosed with Retinitis Pigmentosa, a condition that led to progressive blindness. Undeterred, Clarke pursued his passion for art, graduating with a BA Honours degree in model making. His first career as a dental model maker ended due to his vision loss, prompting him to reinvent himself ‚Äî not as someone who left art behind, but as someone who redefined it entirely.

The Artistic Evolution

Clarke's fascination with dots began with pointillism ‚Äî but as his sight dimmed, his world of dots became literal. He began "seeing through a thousand dots" and turned this experience into an artistic superpower. By merging oversized Braille symbols with vibrant colors and tactile textures, Clarke created a visual language that bridges sight and touch. His unique 26-color Braille alphabet allows audiences to decode hidden messages within the work ‚Äî transforming each piece into a layered, interactive experience.

Advocacy & Mission

Beyond galleries, Clarke brings Braille to life in classrooms, museums, and public installations. Dressed in a Braille-patterned suit, he has engaged over 15,000 children in sensory workshops that inspire empathy and inclusion. Through books, talks, and exhibitions, Clarke shows that vision isn't just about sight ‚Äî it's about perspective.
